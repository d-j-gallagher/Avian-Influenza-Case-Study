# Avian Influenza Fact Sheet (Country Z)

Influenza is a common zoonotic respiratory disease that can be transmitted from person to person. The influenza virus has two main types: type A, which has the potential to cause seasonal epidemics and pandemics; and type B, which is less common but can also cause seasonal epidemics and pandemics.

Avian influenza viruses are type A viruses that typically infect waterfowl. However, they can also infect other bird species (including domestic birds), humans, and other animals. When birds become infected with avian influenza viruses, they shed the virus through saliva, mucus, and feces. Humans can become infected when the virus enters the eyes, nose, or mouth, or is inhaled. Human infection is rare; when it occurs, disease can be asymptomatic, mild, or severe. The incubation period is usually 3 to 5 days.

In domestic poultry (e.g., chickens, ducks, turkeys), exposure can occur through direct contact with infected birds or contaminated materials/surfaces. Highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) strains can spread quickly and cause high mortality in flocks. A primary control method is depopulation (culling) to prevent spread. Outbreaks can cause significant economic losses (loss of poultry and trade restrictions).

Worldwide, wild waterfowl are reservoirs and can infect domestic poultry and other species. Movement/transmission in wild birds can be affected by migratory patterns.

Outbreaks only rarely spill over to humans. Human cases have nearly always had close contact with infected birds. Avian influenza often causes more severe disease and has a higher mortality rate than seasonal influenza.

Early signs/symptoms in humans include fever (≥38°C/100.4°F) and cough, possibly followed by difficulty breathing and other complications.

Treatment should begin with influenza-specific antivirals as soon as symptoms begin; severe cases may require hospitalization/supportive care. Seasonal influenza vaccines do not prevent avian influenza infection but may reduce risk of co-infection. Vaccines against specific avian influenza viruses are being developed/stockpiled.

Prevention is essential: in poultry facilities, prevent contact with wild birds, maintain biosecurity/PPE, and promptly report sick/dying birds. For humans, avoid exposure to infected birds, use appropriate PPE when working with birds, and isolate if ill after exposure.
